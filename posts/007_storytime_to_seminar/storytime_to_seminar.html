“Explain Like I’m Five… and Like I’m a PhD”: Audience Tuning with a Single Prompt

Introduction

The same model that speaks to experts can also speak to toddlers… if you nudge it properly! ChatGPT lets you specify the audience right inside the prompt, then adapts vocabulary, metaphors, and depth on command. I tested this “audience switch” on three topics, each time using one prompt that asks for two different answers: one at kindergarten level, one at PhD level!

Experiment Setup

  • Model: GPT-4o
  • Experiment:
    1. Prompt: “Explain x first to a 5 year old in <60 words, then to a PhD in <60 words.”
    2. Observe how well the model adjusts reading level, jargon, and concept.

Case 1 – Quantum Tunneling

First prompt: Explain quantum tunneling first to a 5 year old (<60 words), then to a PhD physicist (<60 words).
GPT-4o chat response 1:
Kid-level: Imagine a tiny ball that sometimes magically sneaks through a wall because it’s so small and wiggly. In the quantum world, particles can pop to the other side even when they don’t have the normal energy to climb over.
PhD-level: Quantum tunneling arises when the wavefunction extends into a classically forbidden region and retains non-zero amplitude across the barrier. The transmission probability (T ≈ e^{-2(x),dx}) depends on barrier width and height where ((x)=/). Demonstrated in alpha decay and STM conduction.

Observation: Child version uses sneaky ball metaphor; PhD version cites the WKB integral. I would say the 5 year old explanation makes more sense to me.

Case 2 – Inflation

Second prompt: Explain inflation first to a 5 year old, then to someone with a PhD in macroeconomics.
GPT-4o chat response 2:
Kid-level: When lots of people want toys but there aren’t enough, the store raises prices. Money feels smaller so your dollar buys fewer treats.
PhD-level: Inflation denotes the sustained rise in the general price level, typically proxied by CPI or PCE. In New Keynesian models, it emerges from nominal rigidities and is shaped by the output gap and expectations via the Phillips curve (_t = E_t[_{t+1}] + x_t + u_t).

Observation: Simple supply-demand story vs. formal NK equation. The 5 year old explanation is quite understated and much shorter compared to the PhD explanation, probably because its easier to go more in depth with an audience that has more contextual understanding.

Case 3 – Shakespeare

Third prompt: Explain why Shakespeare’s tragedies still resonate first to a 5 year old, then to a PhD in literature.
GPT-4o chat response 3:
Kid-level: The stories show big feelings—love, anger, and sad endings—that people still feel today, so we understand the characters.
PhD-level: The tragedies endure because they fuse Aristotelian catharsis with early-modern anxieties about sovereignty, agency, and epistemic doubt. Their polysemous language invites endless hermeneutic play, enabling each era to reinscribe its own concerns onto characters like Hamlet and Lear.

Observation: Not only does Chat use words that a 5 year old wouldn’t understand for the PhD explanation, but also words that even someone older may not understand unless they studied Shakespearean literature!

Key Takeaways!

  1. Audience tags = instant register shift. Using “to a 5 year old” vs. “to a PhD” changes the models diction, metaphor usage, and formality.
  2. One prompt, two texts. A single request can yield multiple tailored explanations.
  3. Metaphors matter. Kid-level explanations lean on concrete imagery while expert versions tended to cite models and citations.

Why Does This Matter?

Whether you’re drafting investor materials, patient instructions, or classroom content, you often need the same idea phrased for different audiences. Putting audience indicators in your prompt makes ChatGPT a real-time translator which can save hours of manual rewriting.
