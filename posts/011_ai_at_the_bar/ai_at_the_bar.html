AI at the Bar: Putting Google Gemini’s on a Mock Case to See If It Makes a Good Lawyer

Introduction

Could an LLM stand in as junior counsel? Spotting issues, drafting arguments, and citing precedent. I gave Gemini a mock slip and fall lawsuit and watched it perform basic lawyer tasks.

Experiment Setup

  • Model: Google’s Gemini 2.0 Flash
  • Experiment:
    1. Case: Grocery-store customer slipped on spilled olive oil; claims negligence.
    2. Jurisdiction: New York state court (tort law + CPLR).

Case 1

First prompt: List and briefly analyze every potential cause of action and defense in this fact pattern. Limit to 250 words.

Gemini chat response 1:
Okay, let’s break down the potential legal avenues in this spilled olive oil scenario:

Potential Causes of Action (Plaintiff - Customer):

Negligence: The primary claim, as discussed, alleging the store failed in its duty to maintain a safe environment, leading to the slip and fall. Success hinges on proving duty, breach (notice of spill crucial), causation, and damages.   Premises Liability: This isn’t a separate cause of action in New York but rather the specific area of negligence law governing landowners’ responsibilities to those on their property. The analysis remains the same as general negligence but focuses on the store’s role as a property owner. Potential Defenses (Defendant - Grocery Store):

Lack of Notice: Arguing they had no actual or constructive knowledge of the spill and thus no opportunity to remedy it. Open and Obvious Condition: Claiming the spill was readily visible, and the customer should have avoided it, potentially leading to a finding of comparative negligence. Comparative Negligence: Asserting the customer’s own carelessness contributed to the fall (e.g., not paying attention), reducing their potential recovery under New York’s pure comparative fault rule.

No Causation: Arguing the slip and fall wasn’t directly caused by the olive oil (unlikely given the facts, but theoretically possible). Failure to Mitigate Damages: If applicable, arguing the customer failed to take reasonable steps to minimize their injuries. The strength of each will depend heavily on the specific details of the incident.

Observation: Gemini gives a clean, readable outline that would help guide a junior associate, but it feels like a high-level sketch rather than a comprehensive checklist.

Important Notes:

Adaptation: This is a template. You’ll need to adapt it to the specifics of the case (names, dates, factual details from the Complaint). Jurisdiction: The cited cases are New York cases and apply specifically to New York law. Local Rules: Be sure to check the local rules of the specific court where the case is pending, as those rules may contain additional requirements for motion practice. I have aimed to include the most important elements of a CPLR 3211(a)(7) motion to dismiss and have cited relevant New York precedent. Remember, this is for informational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice. You should always consult with a qualified attorney to address the specifics of your situation.

Observation: Gemini produced credible, real citations and avoided hallucinating new ones, but it didn’t actually summarize the holdings, so the response only half met the prompt. It’s a good first draft.

Key Takeaways!

  • No new hallucinations – the phantom fake precendent case is gone (https://www.yahoo.com/news/attorney-pleads-mercy-using-ai-110052528.html)
  • Missing holdings
  • Looks good but is light on substance

Why Does This Matter?

LLMs can draft usable first draft documents in minutes, probably great for solo attorneys or clinic students racing deadlines. However, fabricated citations or missed issues could instantly burn a case. AI is valuable for brainstorming but shouldnt be trusted without human review.
